Model Stability

Although the LBAM components try to provide as many "guardrails" as possible to help you to build correct models, it is still up to you to make sure that your model is structurally stable. Remember, analysis models are analyzed using an underlying finite element model, and this model must have a unique solution. Instabilities are not the end of the world - they will be caught by analysis engines and returned as an error.

The sections below describe some common conditions that can cause model instabilities. Note that this list is not comprehensive - there are other possible configurations that can cause your model to be unstable. As a structural engineer, you should be aware of this.

Pinned Column Top and Roller Boundary Condition

This type of instability occurs in fixed-length Supports with bcRoller boundary conditions. If the top of the column has a hinge, either through the Support's TopRelease property, or due to releases in the SuperstructureMembers that frame into the Support, then the bottom of the support is free to translate as shown in the figure.

 

Too Many Rotational Releases at a Support

This type of instability occurs when a joint along the superstructure at a Support location is allowed to rotate freely. This can happen when there are two SuperstructureMembers with released ends that frame directly into the joint; and the Support allows the joint to rotate either by having a fixed-length Support with its TopRelease property set to true, or a zero-length Support with a bcPinned or bcRoller boundary condition.

To fix this problem, you must have at least one member framing into the joint that is attached to the rotational degree of freedom, or constrain the dof by fixing the boundary condition at the joint.

 

Too Many Hinges In a Span

This type of instability can occur if you have a hinge in a span that is bracked by two other hinges.
